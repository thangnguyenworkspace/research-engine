## Material

### National Law Review — "AI Notetaking Tools Under Fire: Lessons from the Otter.ai Class Action Complaint" (fetched directly, 2026-06-25)

- **Case name:** *Brewer v. Otter.ai*.
- **Date filed:** August 2025.
- **Court:** federal court in California (the article does not name the specific district; the corroborating NPR/legal coverage below identifies it as the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California).
- **Plaintiff:** is "not an Otter accountholder."
- **Core allegations (quoted from the article):** Otter's tool "Joins Zoom, Google Meet, and Microsoft Teams meetings as a participant and transmits conversations to Otter in real time for transcription"; "Records meeting participants' conversations even if they are not Otter accountholders"; and "Uses those recordings to train Otter's automatic speech recognition (ASR) and machine learning models." The complaint characterises Otter as "an unauthorized third party eavesdropper, intercepting communications and repurposing them for product training without consent."

### NPR — "Class-action suit claims Otter AI secretly records private work conversations" (2025-08-15) — search-digest read; direct fetch timed out

- Reported as a federal class action in California: Otter.ai was "hit with a federal class action in California alleging that it secretly recorded private conversations and repurposed them to train its machine learning models."
- Lead plaintiff Justin Brewer (San Jacinto, CA), filed 15 August 2025; he was not an Otter user but was on a sales call where another participant had OtterPilot running.
- Statutes invoked per the coverage: federal Electronic Communications Privacy Act, Computer Fraud and Abuse Act, and California's Invasion of Privacy Act (all-party-consent recording law).
